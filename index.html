💷 What are pay enhancements?

Pay enhancements are extra payments on top of your basic salary for working unsociable hours. They're a significant part of most NHS workers' total pay — but they're taxed just like regular income, so they affect your take-home more than people expect.

📅 What counts as enhanced pay?

Nights: Shifts between approximately 8pm–6am typically attract a 30–37% enhancement
Weekends: Saturday and Sunday shifts usually get a 30% uplift
Bank holidays: Often double time or a flat day's extra pay

The exact rates depend on your pay band, trust, and contract type (Agenda for Change, medical and dental, or local contract).
🧾 How to find your annual enhancement total

The easiest method: check your most recent payslip for a line labelled "enhancements", "unsocial hours", or "weekend allowance". Multiply that monthly figure by 12 for an annual estimate.

Or check your P60 — total pay minus your basic contracted salary = total enhancements for the year.

⚠️ Why it matters for tax
Enhancements are fully taxable. If they push your total income over a threshold (£50,270 for 40%, or £100,000 for the taper), you can suddenly face a much higher marginal rate on every extra shift you pick up. SmartShiftUK includes them in the calculation so you see the true picture.

🇬🇧 What is a UK tax year?

In the UK, the tax year does not run January to December like in many countries. Instead it runs from 6 April to 5 April the following year.

So the current tax year is 6 April 2025 → 5 April 2026, often written as 2025/26.

HMRC (the UK tax authority) resets everything on 6 April each year — your personal allowance, your tax bands, and how much you've earned. That's why we ask how many shifts you've done since 6 April 2025, not since January.

Example: If you arrived in the UK in September 2025 and have done 8 locum shifts since then — enter 8. Shifts from your home country or before 6 April 2025 don't count.

💡 Not sure? Check your payslips or locum agency statements. They'll show the payment dates — count any paid on or after 6 April 2025.

🏦 How the NHS Pension actually works

The NHS Pension is one of the most valuable workplace pensions in the country — but most people only see the deduction on their payslip and feel short-changed. Here's the full picture.

💰 You contribute — but so does your employer (a lot)

When you pay your pension contribution (e.g. 9.8% of your salary), your NHS employer adds roughly 23.7% on top. You never see this money — it goes directly into your pension — but it's genuinely part of your total pay package.

Example: On a £46,000 salary, you pay ~£4,508/year into your pension. Your employer quietly adds another ~£10,902. Your pension is being funded by £15,410/year — not just your contribution.

📐 It's a Career Average (CARE) scheme — not a pot of money
The NHS pension isn't like a savings account where your contributions sit in a fund. Instead, each year of service earns you a guaranteed annual pension in retirement equal to 1/54th of your pensionable pay that year. These annual slices add up across your career. The pension is guaranteed by the government and rises with inflation every year — something private pensions can't promise.

🧾 Your pension contribution reduces your tax bill too

Your pension is deducted from your salary before tax is calculated. This means you get tax relief on every pound you contribute.

On a 9.8% contribution at 40% tax: for every £100 you contribute, HMRC effectively subsidises £40 of it. Your actual out-of-pocket cost is only £60.

This is also why NHS pension contributions count towards your Adjusted Net Income — paying into your pension can bring you below the £100,000 taper threshold, potentially saving you from the 60% effective rate.

⚠️ Should I opt out?
Opting out means you keep your contribution now, but you lose your employer's 23.7% contribution permanently — there's no way to reclaim those years later. It's rarely the right move unless you're facing serious short-term financial pressure. If you're near the Annual Allowance limit (£60,000/year total pension input), speak to an independent financial adviser before opting out.

💡 Every bank and locum shift you do also earns NHS pension — not just your salaried hours. The pension accrual applies to all pensionable NHS pay.

🔢 What is a UK Tax Code?

Your tax code tells your employer how much of your salary is tax-free each year. HMRC (the UK tax authority) assigns it automatically and sends it to your employer.

How to find your tax code: Look at your most recent payslip — it will be printed near the top, usually next to "Tax Code" or "PAYE reference". You may also find it in a letter from HMRC called a P2 or P45.

Common codes and what they mean:

🟢 1257L — The standard code. You get the full personal allowance (£12,570 tax-free). Most people have this.

🔴 BR — "Basic Rate". Every pound you earn is taxed at 20% — no personal allowance. Very common for anyone new to NHS employment, or bank staff starting a second job. You're likely overpaying tax and will get a refund later.

🟡 W1 or M1 — Emergency tax. Each pay period is taxed independently instead of cumulatively. Also common when starting a new NHS job.

🔵 S1257L — Scottish tax rate. The S prefix means you live in Scotland and HMRC is applying Scottish income tax rates.

🔴 K code (e.g. K497) — HMRC is recovering previous underpaid tax. More complex — check your P2 letter.

💡 New to NHS employment? If you've just started a new NHS job and HMRC doesn't have your full tax history yet, they'll usually put you on BR or an emergency code first. This means you're paying too much tax initially — it gets corrected once your P45 from your previous employer is processed. Enter BR if that's what's on your payslip.
